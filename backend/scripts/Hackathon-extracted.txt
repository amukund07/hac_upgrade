Folk lore 1
--- PAGE 2 ---
Come closer, beta… sit here by my feet. Let me pull this shawl over your shoulders; the 
night air is turning cool, and the scent of the parched earth meeting the first drops of rain is 
beginning to rise. Do you smell it? That is the breath of the ancestors. 
Listen carefully, child, for the story I am about to tell you is not found in any of your heavy 
schoolbooks. My own grandmother told it to me while we shelled peas under the shade of 
the old neem tree, her hands moving like tireless weavers as she spoke. It is a story about a 
man who thought he could outrun the wind and a village that knew how to listen to the soil. 
In a valley not too far from here, tucked between two sapphire-blue hills, there was a village 
where life moved like a slow-flowing river. The people there were not rich in gold, but they 
were wealthy in time. They knew the language of the birds, they knew which herb could cool 
a fever, and they never began a harvest without first offering a handful of grain back to the 
earth. 
In this village lived a young man—restless, ambitious, and strong. His hands were made for 
the plow, but his heart was fixed on the horizon. He looked at the elders sitting under the 
banyan tree and saw only stagnation. He looked at the slow rituals of cleaning the 
community wells and the long, patient hours spent weaving straw mats, and he felt a fire of 
frustration. 
"Why do we wait for the seasons?" he would ask, his voice sharp like a new sickle. "Why do 
we let the water flow where it wants? If we dug deeper, if we took more, if we moved faster, 
we would never know hunger again." 
The elders would only smile, their faces lined like the bark of the very trees they protected. 
One old woman, whose eyes were clouded with age but bright with sight, whispered to him, 
"Beta, the earth gives what is needed, not what is craved. If you pull a fruit before it is ripe, 
you lose the sweetness. If you take from the river without thanking the mountain, the 
mountain will eventually forget to weep for you." 
But the young man would not listen. He left the valley, seeking the glittering cities he had 
heard of. 
Years turned into a decade. The village continued its rhythm—the sound of temple bells at 
dawn, the smell of fresh cow-dung cakes burning in the hearth, and the collective song of 
women planting rice in the knee-deep mud. They lived in a circle, where everyone took only 
what they could carry and gave back what they could spare. 
One summer, the young man returned. But he was no longer the boy with the restless heart; 
he was a man who smelled of soot and metal. He brought with him tools the villagers had 
never seen—iron teeth that could tear the earth faster than any bullock, and a hunger that 
seemed to have grown ten times larger. 
He had become wealthy in the world outside, but his eyes were like dry wells. He told the 
villagers that their ways were "ancient" and "broken." He convinced many that the old grove 
of trees at the edge of the village—the sacred grove where no one ever cut a branch 
because it held the spirits of the forest—was a waste of space. 
--- PAGE 3 ---
"The wood is thick," he argued. "We can sell it. We can build bigger houses. We can store 
more than we can eat, and then we will be masters of our own fate." 
Slowly, the greed he carried began to infect the air. The community, once a single fabric, 
began to fray into individual threads. Neighbors who once shared their surplus milk began to 
lock their gates. The collective cleaning of the wells stopped, as everyone waited for 
someone else to do the labor. 
And then, they cut the sacred grove. 
The day the last ancient tree fell, the birds went silent. It was a silence so heavy it felt like a 
physical weight on the chest. That night, for the first time in memory, the village felt cold, 
despite the midsummer heat. 
The young man was triumphant. His granaries were full. His house was the tallest. But that 
monsoon, the sky turned a bruised, angry purple. The rains did not come as a gentle 
blessing; they came as a vengeful torrent. Without the roots of the sacred grove to hold the 
hillside, the earth began to slide. The water, which used to be guided into the clay tanks by 
the communal labor of the village, now ran wild, carving deep scars into the fields and 
washing away the topsoil. 
The young man woke to find his grand house shivering. The heavy iron tools he prided 
himself on were swallowed by the mud. He ran outside, screaming for help, but his 
neighbors were busy trying to save their own walls. The "community" he had mocked was no 
longer there to catch him. 
He ended up huddled in the ruins of a small mud hut belonging to the very old woman who 
had warned him years ago. Her roof leaked, and she had nothing but a single lamp and a 
pot of watery khichdi, but she sat with the same stillness she always had. 
"Where is your wealth now, beta?" she asked softly, her voice barely a whisper against the 
roar of the rain. 
The man wept. He wept for the trees that were gone, for the neighbors who were now 
strangers, and for the hollow silence in his own soul. "I thought I was making us better," he 
sobbed. "I thought speed was progress." 
The old woman placed a withered hand on his head. "Progress without a pulse is just a slow 
death, child. You forgot that you are not the master of the earth, but its guest. A guest does 
not tear up the floorboards of the house that feeds him." 
As the rains subsided, the village was a ghost of its former self. But in the mud, something 
happened. The young man, stripped of his pride and his gold, went to the site of the sacred 
grove. He didn't bring iron tools. He brought his bare hands. He began to clear the debris, 
not for himself, but to find the small saplings that were struggling to breathe under the silt. 
One by one, the other villagers joined him. They didn't speak. They worked in the rhythm of 
the old days—shoulders touching, breath mingling. They realized that the "ancient" wisdom 
was not a burden, but a map. They cleaned the wells together. They shared the little grain 
that remained. They learned that a full stomach is nothing if the heart is lonely. 
--- PAGE 4 ---
It took many years for the grove to return, and the man grew old in its shadow. He never 
became the "master" of the valley again. Instead, he became its servant. He taught the 
children how to read the clouds and why the first fruit of every tree must always be left for 
the birds. 
 
You see, beta... the moral of this story is that greed is a fire that consumes the very hand 
that feeds it. True wisdom lies in balance—knowing that we are woven into the world around 
us, not placed on top of it. Kindness and community are the only currencies that don't lose 
value when the storm hits. 
In your world today, everyone is running. Everyone wants the fastest phone, the biggest car, 
the most likes. They want to cut down the "groves" of their own peace to build monuments to 
their ego. But tell me, child... when the rains of life eventually come—and they always do—
will you have a community to stand with you in the mud? Or will you be standing alone in a 
tall house with a hollow heart? 
Think about it while you sleep. Now, go... the lamp is flickering. Sweet dreams, my little one.

Folk lore 2
--- PAGE 8 ---
The evening comes soft to our village. First the parrots hush in the peepal tree. Then 
the goats shuffle home in dusty clouds. Cooking fires wake, breathing cumin, lentils, 
fresh rotis into the lanes. Above tiled roofs, sunset gold fades to deep blue sky. First 
star blinks like a secret. I light our brass lamp here in the courtyard. Flame dances in 
still air thick with jasmine and damp earth. Pull your mat close—the story waits for 
ready ears. 
You ask why old tales matter when fields call and roofs leak. Tonight I answer not 
with dry words, but a tale from my girlhood. Let the flame hold your eyes. 
Long ago, when I was small as you, village baked under late rains. Ponds sank low. 
Soil cracked like old skin. Seeds sat in baskets, but sky gave nothing. Headman called 
farmers to banyan at dawn. Grain stores thinned—what if clouds held back longer? 
One brother heard while coming from fields. Tired, proud, he grumbled, "Old men 
talk while work waits. Rain comes or not—plough now!" Dawn found him sowing 
after tease-drizzle wet earth. Green shoots rose quick. He walked proud among them. 
"See? Time gained!" 
His brother heard same call. Tired too, but washed, tied scarf, walked to banyan. 
Stood back. Listened. 
Old farmer spoke there, face like dried riverbed, who'd seen more dry spells than us 
all. "Hold seed three days," he said. "Wind wrong. Ants climb hill with eggs. Cranes 
fly straight, no turn. Shallow rain tricks—wait southwest clouds." 
Some laughed low. Quiet one said nothing. Just heard. 
Tease-rain dried fast. Sun hardened earth. First brother's shoots wilted, bent, died. 
Quiet one sowed little to test, kept rest safe in clay jar wrapped tight. 
Third day, wind turned cool, wet, carrying river-weed scent from afar. Clouds piled 
low, heavy. Midnight, monsoon broke—beat roofs, rushed lanes, filled ponds, soaked 
fields deep to roots. Dawn, quiet one sowed into ready earth. Crop rose tall, green. 
First brother's? Patchy, weak, little left to plant. 
You frown now? First brother worked harder—why lose? 
Work without ear is water poured in cracked pot. Effort walks with wisdom, or falls 
alone. 
What saved quiet one? Luck? No. Patience? Yes. More—humility. World speaks many 
voices: elders, wind, ants, clouds, silence. Stories show this again and again. Fool 
rarely knows nothing. Thinks he needs no one. Quiet one trusted signs beyond his 
own strong hands. 
--- PAGE 9 ---
I remember sitting here same as you, interrupting my own grandmother. "But why 
wait?" I demanded. She smiled, touched lamp wick. "Child, stories aren't market 
lists. They're fields you walk slow. Notice dry ground, bird paths, hidden wells. Hurry 
misses roots." 
She lifted small clay lamp, blackened from years. "This is lamp of listening. Burns 
only when someone sits still to learn. Tale isn't play. Carries memory across 
generations. Warns without scold. Teaches without force. Lets you see consequences 
before life carves them deep." 
Someone whispers, "Every story has moral like label?" 
No, child. Some clear as river stone. Some hide like roots underground. Honesty, 
greed, courage, respect—listener uncovers. That's why tales wake mind and heart 
together. 
Moon rises now, silvering courtyard edges. Lamp casts long shadows over mat. 
Tomorrow I'll tell of jackal who borrowed another's voice and lost his own. You'll see 
foolishness wears many faces—not just haste, but pretense too. 
From tonight, when tale reaches you, don't ask first, "True or not?" Ask, "What truth 
does it carry?" Folktale gives no dates, no measures. Gives map for inner life—
choices, regrets, quiet strengths. 
Village breathes with us—cattle in sheds, neem leaves in breeze, crickets hidden in 
grass, women at well, all joined in this circle. Stories survive not because old, but 
because each generation sees own face in them. 
Wisdom lives not just in quick hands or strong will, but in ears open to elders, 
nature, and silence. Listen first, act second. 
Stories teach values through lives lived in tale—pride versus humility, haste versus 
patience—showing how world speaks if we hear beyond our own voice.

Folk lore 3
--- PAGE 13 ---
Come closer, beta... move a little further from the draft of the window. The moon is 
hidden behind the clouds tonight, isn’t it? My mother used to say that when the moon 
hides, it is so the stars can tell their own stories. 
Tonight, I will tell you a story about a weaver and a bird. It is a story about patience 
and the hidden threads that connect a person's soul to their work. 
In a village perched on the edge of a great, whispering forest, lived a weaver whose 
hands were as gnarled as the roots of an old oak. This weaver did not make ordinary 
cloth. It was said that if you wore a shawl made by these hands, you felt the warmth 
of a thousand suns and the coolness of a mountain spring. But the weaver was a 
man of few words; he lived alone in a hut where the only music was the clack-clack-
clack of his wooden loom. 
The villagers were often frustrated with him. "Why does it take you a full moon cycle 
to weave one single sari?" they would grumble, holding out their coins. "The 
merchants in the town use machines; they produce hundreds in a day! You are 
losing gold, old man." 
The weaver would only look at them with eyes that seemed to see something far 
away. "A machine can copy a pattern," he would say softly, "but it cannot hold a 
prayer. A thread pulled in haste is a thread that will soon break." 
In that same village lived a young girl, the daughter of a wealthy landowner. She was 
beautiful, but her heart was like a parched field—always wanting more, never 
satisfied. For her wedding, she demanded a veil that was lighter than air and 
sparkled like the morning dew. She went to the weaver and threw a bag of silver on 
his porch. 
"I want it in seven days," she commanded. "I do not care how you do it, but it must 
be the finest in the land." 
The weaver looked at the silver, then at the girl’s restless eyes. "The silk must be 
washed in the river seven times," he explained. "It must dry in the scent of the 
jasmine flowers. Only then can I begin. Seven days is not enough for the silk to learn 
how to be a veil." 
--- PAGE 14 ---
The girl laughed, a sound like glass breaking. "Money makes time move faster, old 
man. Finish it, or I will have your loom burned." 
Fearful for his only companion—his loom—the weaver began to work. He skipped 
his morning prayers. He did not talk to the birds that sat on his windowsill. He 
stopped listening to the rhythm of the forest. He pulled the threads tight, his fingers 
trembling with the rush. He worked through the nights by the light of a single, 
sputtering candle, his eyes burning with exhaustion. 
On the sixth night, a small, dull-colored bird flew into his hut and perched on the 
loom. It began to sing a melody so mournful, so deep, that the weaver’s hands froze. 
The song sounded like the crying of the earth when the trees are cut, or the sigh of a 
mother whose child has forgotten her. 
The weaver looked down at the veil. In his haste, he had snapped a tiny thread. To 
anyone else, it was invisible. But to him, it was a wound. He realized that in rushing 
to satisfy the girl's greed and his own fear, he had lost the "soul" of the cloth. It was 
just fabric now—cold and dead. 
He stood up, walked to the porch, and threw the bag of silver into the tall grass. 
The next morning, the girl arrived with her servants. "Is it done?" she demanded. 
The weaver handed her a bundle of raw, unspun silk. "The veil is not ready," he said, 
his voice now steady and calm. "And it will not be ready for a year. You cannot buy 
the wind, and you cannot command the heart to beat faster than it is meant to." 
The girl was furious. She ordered her servants to tear down his hut. They broke his 
windows and threw his pots, but when they approached the loom, a strange thing 
happened. Thousands of birds—the same dull-colored birds from the forest—
swarmed the hut, their wings creating a wall of beating hearts. They did not attack; 
they simply stood guard. 
The girl stopped, trembling. She looked at the weaver, who was sitting amidst his 
broken belongings, holding a single thread of silk with such tenderness, such 
reverence, that she felt a sudden, sharp pain in her chest. She realized she had 
everything—gold, silk, servants—but she had never looked at anything in her life 
with the love the weaver had for a piece of string. 
--- PAGE 15 ---
She sat down on the dirt floor and put her head in her hands. "Why am I so empty?" 
she whispered. 
The weaver walked over and placed the raw silk in her hands. "Because you are 
trying to grab the water of the river with an open hand, beta. To hold anything of 
value, you must first learn to be still. You must learn that the beauty is not in the 
finished veil, but in the patience of the weave." 
The girl did not have him punished. Instead, she stayed. For one year, she sat by the 
weaver. She learned how to wash the silk in the river. She learned how to wait for 
the jasmine to bloom. She learned that the clack-clack-clack of the loom was the 
heartbeat of the universe. 
When the veil was finally finished, it wasn't just a piece of clothing. It was a story of a 
year spent in silence, in nature, and in humility. On her wedding day, she didn't look 
like a rich girl; she looked like a piece of the morning light. 
 
My dear child, this story teaches us the value of patience and the sanctity of our 
labor. In your world, everything is "instant." Instant food, instant messages, instant 
success. But remember—anything grown in a hurry has no roots. 
The most beautiful things in life—love, wisdom, and art—require us to slow down 
and put our soul into the threads. If you rush through your days, you are only 
reaching the end faster. But if you weave them with care, you create a tapestry that 
even the wind cannot tear. 
Tell me, beta... in your hurry to reach tomorrow, have you forgotten to look at the 
colors of today?

Folk lore 4
--- PAGE 19 ---
Listen carefully, child... move your stool away from the shadows and sit where the light of the 
lamp can reach you. Do you hear the crickets in the fields? They are the village’s evening 
choir, reminding us that even the smallest voice has a place in the dark. 
Tonight, I want to tell you about two brothers and a single clay pot. It is a story about the 
weight of a grudge and the healing power of forgiveness. 
Long ago, in a village where the soil was as red as a sunset, lived two brothers. They shared 
a small patch of land left to them by their father. They farmed together, ate from the same 
metal plate, and sang the same songs during the harvest. In that village, there was a 
tradition: every family kept a "Guest Pot"—a large, beautifully painted clay vessel filled with 
cool water and a bit of jaggery, kept outside the gate for any thirsty traveler to drink from. It 
was a symbol of the village’s heart. 
One summer, a heatwave hit the valley. The wells began to sigh, and the dust turned into a 
choking veil. Tensions rose with the temperature. One afternoon, while the brothers were 
working the parched earth, they began to argue over a small strip of shade under a wilting 
mango tree. 
The argument was small at first, like a tiny spark in dry grass. But words are like seeds, 
beta—once planted in anger, they grow thorns. The younger brother, blinded by the heat, 
accused the older one of taking more water from their shared jug. The older brother, stung 
by the insult, kicked the "Guest Pot" that stood between them. It shattered into a hundred 
jagged pieces. 
"You have broken the heart of our home!" the younger brother screamed. 
"Then I will build a wall where the pot used to be!" the older brother roared back. 
And so they did. They divided the land. They built a tall fence of thorny acacia branches. 
They stopped speaking. The village, which used to feel like one big family, felt a chill even in 
the burning sun. The "Guest Pot" was never replaced. The spot where it sat became a patch 
of dry, cracked earth. 
Years passed. The brothers grew old, their backs bending like the very fence that separated 
them. The younger brother became consumed by his bitterness. Every time he looked at the 
fence, he remembered the broken pot. He felt like he was carrying those sharp clay shards 
inside his own chest. He became lonely and sour, like milk left out in the sun. 
The older brother, on the other side, was also unhappy. His house felt silent and cold. He 
realized that by shutting his brother out, he had locked himself in. 
One evening, a wandering monk arrived in the village. He was dusty, his throat parched, and 
his feet were blistered. He walked to the gate where the brothers lived, but there was no 
"Guest Pot"—only a fence of thorns and two old men sitting on opposite porches, staring at 
the ground. 
The monk sat down in the dust between the two gates. He didn't ask for food. He didn't ask 
for water. He simply began to dig. 
--- PAGE 20 ---
The brothers, curious despite their anger, came to their respective fences. 
"What are you doing, holy man?" the younger one asked. 
"I am digging a grave," the monk replied without looking up. 
The brothers were shocked. "A grave? For whom?" 
"For the man who died here years ago," the monk said. "The man who used to give water to 
strangers. The man who knew that a brother's love is more precious than a strip of shade. 
He died the day the pot broke, and he has been rotting above ground ever since." 
The words hit the brothers like a splash of cold well-water. They looked at each other 
through the thorns of the fence. For the first time in twenty years, they didn't see an enemy; 
they saw a reflection of their own grey hair and tired eyes. 
The older brother reached out and began to pull at the thorny branches of the fence, his 
hands bleeding from the spikes. The younger brother rushed to help him from the other side. 
Together, they tore down the wall they had built with their own pride. 
They didn't say much—sometimes, beta, the heart speaks loudest in silence. They went to 
the potter in the village and bought the largest, finest clay pot he had. They painted it 
together, their shaky hands overlapping. They filled it with the sweetest water and the purest 
jaggery and placed it back at the gate. 
That night, for the first time in two decades, the brothers sat on the same porch and ate from 
the same plate. The heaviness in their chests—the shards of the broken pot—had vanished. 
 
You see, beta... this story tells us that anger is a heavy stone that you carry, thinking you 
are going to throw it at someone else. But in the end, your own hand grows tired and your 
own heart grows cold. 
In your world, people find it very easy to "block" someone or build "walls" behind their 
screens. They think they are protecting themselves. But every wall you build to keep others 
out also keeps you from the warmth of the sun. Kindness is the water that keeps the clay of 
our lives from cracking. 
Tell me, my child... is there a broken pot in your life that is waiting to be mended, or are you 
still adding thorns to your fence? 
Sleep now. The stars are watching, and they never hold a grudge. Shubh ratri, my dear.

Tonight, let us talk about the Silent Guardian of the Ghats. 
In a land where the sun was so fierce it could turn a stone into a coal, there lived an old 
stone-cutter. He didn't build tall towers or grand monuments for the kings. He spent his 
whole life at the bottom of a deep, natural ravine, carving steps into the rock and building 
small, curved walls that looked like the crescent of a new moon. 
His son, a man who had worked in the great quarries of the city, thought his father was

spend your strength building these small walls in a dry gully? When the monsoon comes, the

The stone-cutter didn't stop his chisel. Clink-clink-clink. "A big wall stops the water, beta, but
